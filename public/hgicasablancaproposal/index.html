02 — A Quick Look at Public Reviews

Some Patterns
Already Visible

Before sending this over we read every public review of the hotel we could pull. 116 from TripAdvisor, 100 from Booking, 68 from Google. 284 in total. Four patterns kept coming up across all three sources. None are the answer on their own. Things worth a closer look in the map.

116 TripAdvisor 100 Booking.com 68 Google 284 reviews analysed
01

Hot water and pressure comes up a lot. 24 mentions across the three sources. Mostly clustered around morning shower hours. Could be infrastructure. Could also just be housekeeping drawing on the same hot water at the wrong time. The map would tell us.

02

Guest requests not making it through. Small handful of reviews about asking for water or towels and nothing happening. Low in volume but the tone is rough. Sort of thing a request-tracking system with SLAs sorts out.

03

Review responses are slow. Current median response time on TripAdvisor is around 5 days. Some have stretched to months. Hilton's standard is 48 hours. AI-drafted responses get this down to under an hour.

04

Breakfast and restaurant at peak hours. The most-discussed topic across all three sources. Mostly positive, but a real negative tail at the busy moments. Service slows down when the room fills up. Could be staffing. Could be kitchen flow. Could be supplier ordering. The map would show us.

These are surface signals. What guests notice. The map is what shows the actual cause behind them.

04 — How We Map

What a Flow Chart
Actually Looks Like

Here's the simplest possible example, from a real moment at any hotel: a guest asks for an extra towel.

Guest calls reception
↓
Reception writes it on a notepad
↓
Radios housekeeping
Lost in radio noise. No record. No-one's tracking it.
↓
HK gets to the room "when free"
No SLA. Could be 5 minutes. Could be an hour.
↓
Guest gets towel — eventually

That's it. That's a flow chart.

Once it's drawn out, the broken bits stop being invisible. Now multiply it by 50 things happening every day across the property — check-in, breakfast service, OTA bookings, complaints, room service, maintenance tickets — and you've got a clear picture of where time and money are actually leaking. The fixes start to look obvious.

In this case the fix is

A central guest-request log (WhatsApp / app / shared dashboard) with SLA tracking. One change makes the same friction disappear from dozens of similar workflows. That's what process mapping points at.

05 — Some Examples

Things We
Tend to Surface

Not a prediction list. Just things hotels are putting in right now and getting real value out of. Won't know which actually fit HGI Sud until we look.

01

WhatsApp guest assistant in three or four languages. Arabic, French, English, plus one more if it helps. Handles FAQs, late-checkout asks, restaurant recs, pre-arrival info. Picks up the easy 70% so the team only handles what actually needs them. Probably the single biggest implementation in hotels right now.

02

AI-drafted review responses. Booking, Tripadvisor, Google. Drafts a reply in your voice within an hour of the review going up. You approve in batches. Hilton's standard is 48 hours. This gets it under one.

03

Pre-arrival upsell flow. Personalised emails before arrival. Room upgrade, late checkout, breakfast, airport transfer. Built off booking source and guest history. Adds incremental revenue without anyone having to touch it.

04

Daily reports written for you. Flash, MTD, owner updates. Pulled from the PMS instead of someone assembling them by hand at 06:30. In your inbox before the morning meeting.

05

Predictive maintenance on HVAC and key kit. Smart monitoring that flags failures before guests do. Marriott cut equipment failures 25% rolling this out. Useful at a property with a heavy A/C load.

06

Quiet prep for the HotelKey rollout. Hilton is replacing OnQ across all properties and HGI is in the first wave. The maps make the migration smoother. You'll already know which workflows depend on which systems and where the workarounds live.

07

Centralising things scattered across tools. Maintenance tickets, lost and found, supplier comms, shift notes. Into one place everyone can see. Boring. Always works.

06 — How It Goes

The engagement,
in three movements.

No black box. You're in the room for every call that matters. We map, you decide what to act on, then we build it.

i
Phase one
Map.

A few weeks of observing how the property actually runs.

Short conversations with each head of department. Time on the floor during the moments that stress the operation. Every workflow drawn out as it really happens, not as the SOP says. You see the maps mid-engagement so you can react before anything's locked in.

A clean set of process maps and a marked shortlist of where the friction sits.

ii
Phase two
Decide.

Sit down with you and pick the wins together.

Walk through what we found. Identify the low-hanging fruit. Choose what's worth doing first, based on impact and what you can move on without burning political capital. Some items get owned by your team, some by us.

A prioritised plan, agreed with you, not for you.

iii
Phase three
Build.

Layer in the tools that lock the wins in for good.

WhatsApp guest assistant. AI-drafted review responses. Daily reports written for you. Predictive maintenance. Whichever the maps point at. Built brand-safe, integrated with what you already run, designed to be handed over and lived with by your own team.

Working tools, owned by you. No retainer. No vendor lock-in.
